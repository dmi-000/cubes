❯ could rational angles prevent alignments between multiple cubes needed to     
  form maximal regions?                                                         

⏺ Yes in principle — and demonstrably at small n — but the n=6 evidence points
  the other way. The question splits cleanly on whether the maximum lives on an
  open stratum or on a wall.

  Where rationality costs nothing. The exact count is piecewise constant on a
  finite semialgebraic stratification of configuration space, and SO(3,ℚ) is
  dense in SO(3). So every value attained on a full-dimensional (generic)
  stratum is attained by rational rotations — density plus local constancy. If
  the true maximum is generic, a rational search loses nothing, and the
  quat-scale-512 grid is evidently fine enough to land in those strata (the 635
  record sits on a broad plateau where 192 integer moves don't change the
  count).

  Where it can cost. Maxima can live on lower-dimensional walls — exact
  alignments — and some walls contain no rational points at all. Both halves of
  this are already in our data:

  - n=2 is a proven case. The generic pair count is 4; the shared-diagonal wall
  gives 13. Any wall-avoiding search caps at 4 forever. The count function isn't
  semicontinuous in the helpful direction: approaching a wall you see the
  generic value; the wall value only exists on the wall.
  - n=5 is a suspected case. The golden five-compound (351) requires ℚ(√5) — a
  5-fold axis forces trace 1+2cos 72°, so no rational rotation ever realizes
  that wall, only approaches it. Best random rational total at n=5 in our
  (small) sample: ~317. Whether a generic n=5 config can beat 351 is genuinely
  open.
  - Note some walls are rational-reachable — coincident-plane families like the
  axial and six6 configs are exactly representable at small quat scales. It's
  specifically the irrational-algebraic walls (golden, and anything with 5-fold
  symmetry) that rational search can only orbit.

  Why n=6 looks different. For the deep counts the walls only hurt — that's
  exactly the content of T2, now backed by the census: degeneracy removes
  swap-curve vertices and merges bottom cells (sub-36 config: E−V drops 34→32
  etc.), never adds. For the shallow counts walls can help — the six6 wall
  config has d1 = 120, above the record's 118 — but at ruinous cost to
  everything deeper: its total is 355. Empirically every config ≥625 has the
  generic deep tail (164, 102, 36, 1) and a balanced, generic-looking shallow
  profile. So the trade at n=6 is: an alignment buys a few shallow regions by
  coincidence but each coincident plane-pair collapses whole families of cells.
  Net negative for the total in everything observed — but "no wall beats 635" is
  unproven; T2 only protects d3/d4/d5, and a wall that gained more in d1+d2
  than it merged away below is not excluded by anything we have.

  The concrete testable gap: the strongest candidate irrational wall is the
  golden family itself — e.g., the five-compound plus an exactly-optimized sixth
  cube, counted with the ℚ(√5) engine (which handles it exactly; the rational
  restriction is a property of the search, not of the counting machinery). If
  some irrational 6-configuration beats 635, that neighborhood is where it would
  live. Happy to set that up as a follow-up.

⏺ The ℚ(√5) search is launched: an agent is building golden_six.py on top of the
  existing machinery (which turns out to already compute in ℚ(√5) internally —
  the golden face normals (±φ/2, ±1/(2φ), ±1/2) are exact unit vectors there, so
  the five golden cubes feed straight into the validated counter). It must pass
  three gates (golden five → 351 with sub-compounds 1/13/67/177; rational
  six-config reproduces a known seed; golden five + duplicate cube → 351 through
  the coincident-plane machinery) before searching two families of sixth cubes
  — rational orientations and rational rotations of a golden cube — with
  symmetric candidates, random sampling, and exact hill-climbing, logging
  whether any depth beats its rational record (d1 118, d2 214) or the total 635.

  On whether further extensions could be necessary — yes, provably in general,
  and here's the structure:

  Some number field always suffices, but no single one suffices for everything.
  The count function is constant on finitely many strata, each defined by
  polynomial equations and inequalities over ℚ. Any nonempty such stratum
  contains points with real-algebraic coordinates, so the global maximum is
  always attained at an algebraic configuration — but the field depends on which
  stratum wins.

  Which walls need which fields. The symmetry walls are the informative case: a
  k-fold arrangement of cubes about a common axis needs ℚ(cos 2π/k). That's
  rational for k ∈ {1,2,3,4,6} — the crystallographic cases, which is why the
  13-region pair wall (60° about a shared body diagonal — Rodrigues gives a
  fully rational matrix there) was already reachable by our rational search.
  It's ℚ(√5) for k = 5, 10 (the golden family), ℚ(√2) for k = 8 (the 45°
  face-axis pair wall with 9 regions already needs it — so a second extension is
  demonstrably required for at least one known wall at n=2), ℚ(√3) for k = 12,
  and a cubic field for k = 7 or 9. Coincident-plane walls, by contrast, are all
  rational-reachable. So the natural priority order after ℚ(√5) is ℚ(√2), then
  ℚ(√3), then towers like ℚ(√2,√5) for configurations mixing symmetry types.

  The engine cost is graded. ℚ(√5) → ℚ(√d) for any squarefree d is a
  one-constant change in the Q5 class (the ·5 in multiplication and the sign
  rule). A biquadratic tower like ℚ(√2,√5) needs degree-4 exact sign
  determination — still mechanical (basis 1, √2, √5, √10; interval refinement
  with a separation bound, or resultant signs). Full generality — arbitrary real
  algebraic entries — is what the certified-interval CN design was built to
  anticipate: float intervals filter, exact arithmetic decides, and only the
  exact leaf layer needs generalizing.

  Whether it's worth it is exactly what the golden run will tell us. The census
  results cut against walls for the total: T2-direction evidence says alignments
  only merge deep cells (the sub-36 config lost census vertices; six6 traded d1
  = 120 > 118 for a total of 355). The golden wall is the strongest
  counter-candidate because n = 5 is the one place a wall demonstrably leads
  (351 vs ~317 best random — though that baseline is thin; the n>6 agent's --n
  binary will incidentally give us a proper generic n=5 campaign to firm it up).
  If golden5+1 can't beat 635 even with exact hill-climbing on the sixth cube,
  that's real evidence the rational search space isn't the binding constraint —
  and ℚ(√2)/ℚ(√3) sweeps become low-priority. I'll report when the agent lands.

❯ would cube_regions enhanced to  ℚ(√n,…) be faster than Python engines?        

⏺ Almost certainly yes — likely 10–100× faster than the Python engines, though
  by a smaller margin than the rational C++ engine's current ~100–1000× lead.
  The reason is that the two cost factors are independent, and the one that
  hurts (wider integers) is much smaller than the one you keep (no
  interpreter/object overhead).

  The field penalty is bounded and modest. Going rational → ℚ(√d) turns each
  coordinate into a pair (a, b) meaning a + b√d, and the sign predicate — when
  the two terms disagree in sign — reduces to comparing a² against d·b². That
  squares the operand bit-length: the current predicates sit near 2⁸⁵ (inside
  int128), so ℚ(√d) pushes them past 2¹²⁷ and you need 256-bit integers. A
  depth-2 tower ℚ(√a,√b) makes the sign test nested (sign of p + q√b with p, q ∈
  ℚ(√a) recurses into another squared comparison), needing ~512-bit or a small
  bignum. But 256-bit fixed-width multiply is only a handful of 64-bit mults —
  call it 5–15× per predicate — and predicates are a fraction of total runtime
  (the clipping and cell-dedup combinatorics dominate and are unchanged). So an
  enhanced engine is realistically ~3–10× slower than the rational C++ engine,
  not orders of magnitude.

  The C++-vs-Python gap you keep is the big one. The Python engines are slow
  mostly for reasons that have nothing to do with the field: every number is a
  heap-allocated Q5/Fraction object holding arbitrary-precision ints, and every
  operation pays interpreter dispatch. That's the source of the existing
  ~100–1000× ratio, and a C++ port with fixed-width 256-bit ints avoids
  essentially all of it.

  Concrete anchors (measured/observed): the rational C++ engine counts a 6-cube
  config in ~0.05–0.5 s (the 699 config clocked 0.46 s); a Python ℚ(√5) eval
  runs ~20–30 s (the 681 config took 30.8 s in golden_six); the qtower tower
  evals ran 0.5–27 s. Apply a 5–10× field penalty to the C++ number and you land
  around 0.25–5 s — comparable to or faster than Python's heaviest and roughly
  an order of magnitude faster than its typical.

  Where the margin shrinks, and the real caveat. If deep towers force
  arbitrary-precision bignum rather than fixed 256/512-bit, both sides do bignum
  and C++'s edge narrows toward ~20–50× (still winning on allocation and
  dispatch). More important: the blocker was never speed — it's correctness.
  Signed 256/512-bit multiply plus nested exact tower-sign is exactly the
  error-prone code that QFIELD_SPEC.md gates behind "does a quadratic wall
  actually produce a net win," and the engine only has a partial 256-bit helper
  (for centroids) today.

  Bottom line: worth building only if the field program needs bulk exact ℚ(√d)
  counting — a wall campaign over thousands of configs, where 30 s each in
  Python is prohibitive but 1–3 s each in C++ is fine. For the handful of
  one-off wall verifications we do now, the Python towers are already fast
  enough and don't justify the port's development risk. That matches the current
  gating: if a quadratic wall shows a genuine net gain and you want to search
  its neighborhood at scale, that's the trigger to write it.

❯ can we search all region rich areas?                                          

⏺ Two answers, depending on how literally you mean "all."

  Exhaustively — every region-rich stratum — no. Region-rich areas are the
  walls: the measure-zero strata of the ~15-dimensional configuration space
  SO(3)⁶/(global rotation) where exact incidences among the 36 face-planes
  change the arrangement's combinatorial type. That wall set is a semialgebraic
  arrangement in 15-D whose stratum count is combinatorially explosive, and it
  includes "accidental" incidences (one edge of cube i meeting a face of cube j
  at a special point) that carry no global symmetry and aren't cleanly
  finitely-parameterizable. Enumerating literally all of them isn't feasible.

  The region-rich areas that matter — yes, systematically. The records all live
  on symmetry walls, not accidental ones (golden five = an icosahedral orbit;
  699 = two 3-fold orbits on a shared axis; 655 = two body-diagonal pair-walls),
  and symmetry walls form a finite, enumerable catalog:

  ▎ (finite subgroup G ⊂ SO(3)) × (partition of the 6 cubes into G-orbits) × 
  ▎ (seed orientations mod G).

  The finite subgroups of SO(3) are fully classified — cyclic Cₙ, dihedral Dₙ,
  tetrahedral T, octahedral O, icosahedral I. For each, a cube placed under G
  lands in an orbit whose size is |G|/(symmetry it shares with G), and you
  enumerate the ways 6 decomposes. For example under a 3-fold axis: 6 = 3+3 is
  exactly the 699 family (two C₃ orbits on one axis); 6 = 3+1+1+1, 3+2+1, etc.
  are its siblings; under I, 6 = 5+1 is the golden-wall family (681). Each entry
  is a low-dimensional family (1–6 continuous seed parameters), so
  exact-counting and hill-climbing within it is cheap.

  Crucially, the symmetry type also pins the field, so the catalog tells you
  which engine each family needs: Cₙ → ℚ(cos 2π/n), which is rational for n ∈
  {1,2,3,4,6}, ℚ(√2) for n=8, ℚ(√3) for n=12; T/O → rational; I → ℚ(√5); mixed
  axes → towers ℚ(√a,√b). That's the same taxonomy from QFIELD_SPEC.md, now used
  as a search plan rather than a reaction to walls we stumbled on.

  So "search all region-rich areas" has a concrete, completable form: a 
  symmetry-stratified sweep that walks the subgroup lattice from low symmetry
  (C₂) to high (I), enumerates the 6-cube orbit partitions for each, and
  exact-counts every family in its proper field. It would cover the symmetry
  walls exhaustively — turning the ad-hoc discoveries (655/681/699) into
  systematic entries — instead of finding them by luck.

  Caveats to be honest about:
  - The cyclic tower Cₙ is formally infinite, but a cube only "sees" rotations
  modulo its own octahedral symmetry, so distinct alignments that change the
  count are bounded — you enumerate up to a modest fold, not to infinity.
  - Accidental (non-symmetric) walls are excluded, but the census evidence (T2:
  degeneracy only merges deep cells; records are the symmetric ones) makes them
  very likely sub-maximal — worth stating as an assumption, not a theorem.
  - Within each family the seed parameters are still continuous, so you climb
  inside each entry; but each is only 1–6 dimensional, so this is fast.

  This is a well-defined build — the natural systematic successor to the wall
  finds so far. If you want it, I'd spec a symmetry-stratified search (subgroup
  catalog → orbit partitions → per-family exact count + climb, dispatched to the
  right field engine) and delegate the implementation to the smallest model
  that can handle it. Want me to write that spec?
